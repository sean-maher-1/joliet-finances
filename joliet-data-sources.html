Where the numbers come from

All figures are for the City of Joliet's total primary government (governmental plus business-type activities), in millions of dollars unless noted. They are taken from the city's own audited Annual Comprehensive Financial Reports (ACFRs), fiscal years 2011 through 2024: the Management's Discussion & Analysis condensed statements, the government-wide Statement of Net Position, the capital-assets note, and the enterprise-fund statements. The seven indicators follow the Strong Towns Finance Decoder method.

Method & notes

  • Net financial position is the city's money-like assets (cash, investments, money owed to it, plus deferred outflows) minus everything it owes (total liabilities plus deferred inflows). It equals the audited net position minus net capital assets.
  • The two big step-downs follow accounting rules (GASB 68 in 2015 and GASB 75 in 2018) that first required cities to report their full pension and retiree-health promises. Years before 2015 exclude pensions and years before 2018 exclude retiree health, so the early years sit on a different basis.
  • 2011 and 2012 were read by optical character recognition (OCR) from the 2012 ACFR, which is a scanned document with no text layer. The OCR figures reconcile exactly to the audited net position and capital totals. 2011 water/sewer interest is a small estimate (~$1.6M), and 2021 water/sewer interest is interpolated, because those statement pages are scanned images.
  • 2023 revenue includes a one-time $99.8M donated asset (the Houbolt Road Bridge). The revenue-based ratios use 2023 revenue with that item removed, so the trends stay comparable.
  • The repair-bill estimate restates the audit's accumulated depreciation ($711.6M in 2024) to today's construction prices (×1.75 to ×2.25), giving a deliberately rough $1.25B–$1.6B range.
  • Per-household figures use about 51,055 Joliet households (U.S. Census, ACS 2024).

Headline figures (2024)

  • Net financial position: −$1.24B (about −$24,300 per household)
  • Estimated deferred repair bill: $1.25B–$1.6B (about $24,400–$31,400 per household)
  • Combined, on- and off-the-books: roughly −$2.7B (about −$52,000 per household)
  • What's behind the shortfall: retiree health care (~$500M), pensions (~$390M), and debt (~$447M, mostly water and sewer)
